A kingdom for a toilet!

Responsive web application

Project overview

Design a user-friendly responsive web application for searching toilets, especially for seniors, based on geolocation.

Role

UI/UX designer

Duration

5 weeks

Context

Project within the CareerFoundry UI course

Goal

Intuitive to use when there is time pressure

Challenge

Adaptation for the elderly & no duplication of existing applications

Who we’re up against?

My Internet research showed that much of my competition is native apps. Government entities mainly use web apps and target a national database. So, I chose three applications for my analysis to have a broader view of the competitors: a student project Great British Toilet Map (supported by an IT company), a native application PeeQwiq, and Google Maps.

For whom we design?

User research is a critical stage in design - using the user-centric concept will eliminate financial and time losses resulting from poorly selected functionalities, an overabundance of options, or a poorly selected target group. I chose three people to conduct initial interviews. Based on these, I created 3 personas that will serve as reference points for my application.

my hypothesis

I assume that in times of emergency, it's crucial to gain quick information on where can take care of physiological needs without unnecessary logging and complicated options. Nowadays, the fastest way to get information is to use the Internet.

interviews

I conducted interview face-to-face or through a video interview app.

Interviews were conducted with 3 people.

Individual interviews lasted up to 40 minutes.

The interview contained 8 questions.

findings from the interviews

Frustrations
Lack of information about paying.
Lack of information about cleanliness.
Poor or outdated database.

Needs & Goals
Great need for filtering the search result.
Reliable database.
Fast giving search results.
Be even more organized when traveling or sightseeing.
Faster finding the toilet.
Taking care of your needs in comfortable places.

MVP objective & JTBD

The MVP will only have essential functionalities that will make it possible to test the application. Based on the MVP, I established 4 JTBDs, which I turned into user flows.

JTBD #1
Immediate geolocation

When I really have to pee, I want find the toilet quick, so I can continue my journey/walk in comfort.

JTBD #2
Searching for a toilet by address or area

When I am in a foreign city, I want to know that I always find the toilet, so I can not worry if I drink too much coffee in the morning.

JTBD #3
Clicking on a toilet point displays the information (time to reach the place, the distance).

When I am in a foreign city, I want to know that I always find the toilet, so I can not worry if I drink too much coffee in the morning.

JTBD #4
Search options with different parameters – in this case payed/free filter.

When I find the toilet, I want not to be cappted by the payment option, so I can use this toilet and don’t look for another one.

User flows

Wanting always to provide the largest visible area of the map, I decided to use pop-ups and modals.

First prototype

I prepared sketches for the three user paths and two iterations for each flow.

Let’s test it!

The usability test was attended by 3 people representing my personas. Each of them went through 3 tasks. The test took place using the Zoom application, where users shared their screens with me.

goal

Checking whether user flows have been built well.

tasks

#1 Please find toilets in New York.
You are going to visit New York. You don’t know where you will end up yet, but you want to ensure that you can use the toilet wherever it is. [testing search bar]

#2 How do you find out which toilet is closest to you?
[testing whether the user can see his location on the map and whether he will use the information pinned to the toilet]

#3 You don’t have any cash, only a credit card. How do you find free toilets?
[finding & using filter testing]

Results

To analyze the results, I used Jakob Nielsen's error severity rating system. The most critical problems (3) and less significant ones (2) have been corrected in the first low-fidelity wireframes.

Let’s build the structure

After user testing and forming MVP guidelines, it's time to move from sketches to Figma and build low and mid-fidelity wireframes. Based on two screens, I will show how I made changes between iterations through consultation and conversation with users.

Responsiveness - adding breakpoints

I've started wireframing from the screen for iPhone 13, which is the XS (0-480px) size of the screen. From there, I prepared 4 breakpoints:

Margin: 3% / Gutter: 2% / Column width: 6% / 12 column layout

Creating visual language

I wanted to achieve maximum usability in this project, ease of navigation, and that feeling of "after all, there is nothing special here, but it's easy to use." Something similar to Google Maps, which is not the the peak of design, but it serves its functions perfectly.

So what does it look like in the end?

Simplicity, no oversaturation of elements guarantees no cognitive overload for the user. Immediate user location.

Quick access to filtering option - extensive enough buttons even for the visually challenged.

Eye-catching selection of the use of filtering - contrast ratio tested [7.73:1].

Precise information on how to get to the restroom with the estimated time. Possibility to add advertising in the form of a point sponsor.

Is this the end?

Takeways

For now, yes. My conclusion from the project: most of the activities passed without problems like user testing, there were also small obstacles, like conflicting feedback from fellow students, but I resolved them by re-testing with users.

Thanks to this project, I learned and understood the methodologies in the UX process, tasted face-to-face with users, tried different ways of testing, and looked at the project comprehensively and not just from the perspective of a UI designer. I will use every drop of this knowledge in my next project.

Did I achieve the Goal and cope with the Challenge?

The goal was to create an intuitive application to use when there is time pressure. The answer is YES:

- usability tests showed that the application architecture is simple and intuitive.

The challenge was to adapt the app for older people and no duplicate existing applications:

- usability tests with older people showed that they don't have any problem with clickable elements and with contrast ratio,

- application uses common patterns, but it's not a direct copy of existing apps.
